# Indian Medical Document Formats — Complete Reference Report

> A comprehensive reference covering 48 medical document formats used across the Indian healthcare system, organized into four parts: Medical Certificates, Referral Letters, OPD/Progress Notes, and Operation/OT Notes. Each entry includes where it is used, the sections it contains, what distinguishes it from similar documents, and a realistic example with Indian names, medicines, test values, and institutional context.

## PART A: MEDICAL CERTIFICATES (12 Formats)

---

### A1. Sick Leave Certificate (School / Office)

**Where used:** Issued by a registered medical practitioner (RMP) to justify absence from school, college, or workplace. Required by HR departments, school administrations, and for leave encashment. Commonly requested for 1–7 days of absence due to fever, infection, injury, or post-surgical recovery.

**Sections included:**
- Header with clinic/doctor name, qualifications, registration number
- Patient name, age, gender
- Date of examination
- Diagnosis / presenting complaint
- Recommended rest period (in days)
- Date of resumption
- Doctor's signature, seal, and MCI/State Medical Council registration number

**What makes it different:** Unlike a prescription, it carries no medication orders. Its sole purpose is to authorize absence. Indian employers often require the certificate to be from a government-registered practitioner (RMP) and may reject certificates from alternative medicine practitioners (AYUSH) unless specifically permitted. The State Medical Council registration number is mandatory for validity.

**Example:**

```
Dr. Suresh Patil, MBBS, MD (General Medicine)
Reg. No: MMC-48217 (Maharashtra Medical Council)
Patil Clinic, Shop No. 4, Sai Plaza, Kothrud, Pune – 411038
Phone: 020-2538XXXX

                    SICK LEAVE CERTIFICATE

Date of Issue: 12 June 2026

This is to certify that I have examined Mr. Ramesh Kumar S/o Shri
Dinesh Kumar, aged 34 years, male, on 12 June 2026 at my clinic.

The patient is suffering from Acute Viral Fever with throat infection
(temperature recorded 102.4°F, throat congested, tonsils enlarged).

He is advised rest for 03 (three) days from 12 June 2026 to
14 June 2026 and will be fit to resume work on 15 June 2026.

This certificate is issued on patient's request for submission to
his employer.

Signature: ____________________
(Dr. Suresh Patil, MD Medicine)
Seal of Patil Clinic
```

### A2. Fitness Certificate (Employment / Pre-Employment)

**Where used:** Required by employers (private companies, government departments, PSUs, armed forces, railways) as part of pre-employment onboarding. Also used for annual health check-ups in safety-critical roles (drivers, pilots, factory workers, miners). Issued by empaneled doctors or civil surgeons.

**Sections included:**
- Candidate name, age, gender, father's name
- Post applied for / department
- General physical examination (height, weight, BMI, BP, pulse)
- Systemic examination (CVS, RS, CNS, abdomen)
- Vision test (with and without glasses)
- Hearing test
- Blood investigations (CBC, blood sugar, lipid profile)
- Urine examination
- Chest X-ray findings
- ECG findings
- Declaration of fitness (fit / unfit / fit with restrictions)
- Examining doctor's signature and seal

**What makes it different:** This is a screening document, not a treatment record. It declares fitness for a specific job. Government jobs use a prescribed proforma (e.g., Form 22 for Central Government). The certificate must explicitly state "FIT" or "TEMPORARILY UNFIT" — ambiguity invalidates it. HIV testing is no longer mandatory per Supreme Court guidelines.

**Example:**

```
APOLLO CLINIC, NOIDA
Pre-Employment Medical Fitness Examination
(For: TCS Ltd., Noida Sector 62)

Name of Candidate: Priya Sharma          Age: 26 years   Sex: Female
Father's Name: Shri Anil Sharma

INVESTIGATIONS:
Hb: 12.8 g/dL | TLC: 7,200/cu.mm | Platelets: 2.4 lakh/cu.mm

### A3. Fitness Certificate (Sports / School Admission)

**Where used:** Required for school admission (especially boarding schools, Kendriya Vidyalayas), sports participation (school, district, state, national level), NCC enrollment, and adventure activities (mountaineering, scuba). Issued by pediatricians or general physicians.

**Sections included:**
- Child's name, age, gender, date of birth
- Parent/guardian name
- Purpose (school admission / sports event name)
- Birth height and weight with percentile
- Immunization status (up to date / pending)
- Developmental milestones (for younger children)
- Cardiac fitness (murmur screening)
- Vision and hearing screening
- Any chronic illness / allergy / asthma
- Fitness declaration for the specific activity
- Doctor's signature and seal

**What makes it different:** Pediatric focus with growth parameters referenced to WHO/IAP growth charts. Must specifically clear the child for physical exertion (sports) — a general fitness certificate is often rejected. Asthma and cardiac conditions are specifically screened because they are the leading causes of sudden collapse during sports in India.

### A4. Disability Certificate (Government Format)

**Where used:** Issued by a Medical Board constituted at district hospitals / government medical colleges as per the Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act, 2016 (RPwD Act). Required for availing government benefits: pension, railway concession, job reservation, disability scholarship, and income tax deduction. Uses the standard UDID (Unique Disability ID) format.

**Sections included:**
- Name, age, gender, parent/guardian
- Aadhaar number
- Type of disability (locomotor / visual / hearing / intellectual / multiple)
- Permanent or temporary disability
- Percentage of disability (in multiples of 10, as per guidelines)
- Disability since birth / acquired (date)
- Medical board members' signatures (minimum 3: a civil surgeon or equivalent, a specialist in the relevant field, and one more member)
- UDID number
- Validity (lifelong for permanent; review date for temporary)
- Guidelines reference

**What makes it different:** Requires a Medical Board, not a single doctor. The percentage must follow the Gazette of India guidelines (e.g., 40% or above qualifies for benefits). Uses the UDID portal (swavlambancard.com). Cannot be issued by private practitioners. The format is uniform across India since 2017.

**Example:**

```
GOVERNMENT OF TAMIL NADU
OFFICE OF THE DISTRICT MEDICAL OFFICER
District Disability Medical Board, Coimbatore

UNIQUE DISABILITY IDENTITY CARD (UDID)
[As per RPwD Act, 2016]

Name: Mohammed Iqbal              Age: 42 years    Sex: Male

Cause: Post-polio residual paralysis
Disability since: Birth (acquired in childhood)
Permanent / Temporary: PERMANENT

DISABILITY PERCENTAGE: 60%

The above disability has been assessed as per the Guidelines
for Assessment of Extent of Disability, Gazette of India

MEDICAL BOARD:
1. Dr. S. Kandasamy, MS Ortho     — Chairman
   (Joint Director of Medical & Rural Health Services)

### A5. Death Certificate (Government Format)

**Where used:** Issued by the Registrar of Births and Deaths under the Registration of Births and Deaths Act, 1969. The medical portion (cause of death) is filled by the treating doctor; the civil portion is registered at the local body (municipality / panchayat). Required for: cremation/burial permit, property transfer, bank account closure, pension, insurance claim, and legal heir certificate.

**Sections included:**
- Deceased name, age, gender
- Father's / husband's name
- Address
- Date and time of death
- Place of death (hospital / residence)
- Immediate cause of death
- Antecedent causes (underlying)
- Interval between onset and death
- Manner of death (natural / accidental / suicidal / homicidal — if in doubt, refer to police)
- Whether autopsy performed
- Pregnancy-related (for women) — yes/no
- Tobacco contribution (yes/no/unknown)
- Certifying doctor's name, qualification, signature, registration number
- Hospital name and registration number

**What makes it different:** Legally the most scrutinized certificate. Uses Form 4 (hospital death) or Form 4A (non-institutional death). The cause-of-death section follows ICD-10 structure (immediate + underlying). If the death is within 24 hours of admission or is unnatural, a police intimation (Form 112 / inquest) is mandatory before the certificate is issued. The MCCD (Medical Certificate of Cause of Death) portion is confidential and sent to the Registrar.

**Example:**

```
GOVERNMENT OF KARNATAKA
MEDICAL CERTIFICATE OF CAUSE OF DEATH (Form 4)
[Registration of Births and Deaths Act, 1969]

Hospital: Manipal Hospital, Old Airport Road, Bengaluru

Deceased: Smt. Lakshmi Devi          Age: 68 years   Sex: Female
W/o: Late Shri Venkatesh
Address: #42, 7th Cross, Malleshpalya, Bengaluru – 560075

Date of Death: 09 June 2026
Time of Death: 14:35 hrs
Place of Death: ICU, Manipal Hospital

CAUSE OF DEATH (ICD-10):
Immediate cause: Cardiopulmonary arrest        ~ 30 minutes
Due to: Septic shock with multi-organ          ~ 4 days
        dysfunction syndrome
Due to (underlying): Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus  ~ 18 years
                     with diabetic nephropathy

Manner of death: NATURAL
Autopsy: Not conducted (relatives declined)
Pregnancy-related: No
Tobacco contributed: No

I certify that the above information is true to the best of
my knowledge.

Date: 09 June 2026

Signature: ____________________
(Dr. Anil Menon, MD General Medicine)
Reg. No: KMC-55104

### A6. Birth Certificate (Hospital Issued)

**Where used:** Issued by the hospital where the delivery took place, on Form 11 (institutional birth report). This is the first document; it is forwarded to the local Registrar, who then issues the official Birth Certificate. Required for: school admission, passport, Aadhaar, voter ID, and all age-proof purposes.

**Sections included:**
- Baby's name (if decided) or "Baby of [mother]"
- Sex
- Date and time of birth
- Weight at birth
- Type of delivery (vaginal / LSCS / assisted)
- Place of birth (hospital name and address)
- Mother's name, age, religion, education, occupation
- Father's name, age, religion, education, occupation
- Parents' address
- Number of previous live births / stillbirths to the mother
- Gestational age at birth
- Birth weight and APGAR score
- Attendant at birth (doctor / nurse / dai) with name and registration
- Informant's name and signature (usually father)
- Hospital seal and signature of the medical superintendent

**What makes it different:** The hospital issues a "birth report," not the final certificate — the municipality issues the legal Birth Certificate. Must be filed within 21 days of birth (free registration). After 21 days but within 1 year, a late fee and an affidavit are required; after 1 year, a magistrate's order is needed. APGAR and birth weight are clinical fields unique to this document.

**Example:```

### A7. Medical Fitness for Travel / Visa

**Where used:** Required by certain embassies (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, Qatar, Oman, Bahrain — GCC countries) for employment visas, and by some Western countries for long-stay student visas. Conducted at GAMCA-approved medical centers for GCC countries. Also used for pilgrimage (Hajj, Umrah) fitness.

**Sections included:**
- Applicant name, passport number, nationality, age, gender
- Photograph (fixed to the form)
- Destination country
- Sponsor / employer name
- Physical examination (height, weight, BP, vision, hearing)
- Chest X-ray (specifically for tuberculosis)
- HIV test (mandatory for most GCC countries)
- HBsAg and HCV tests
- Blood group
- Pregnancy test (for women of childbearing age)
- Stool and urine examination
- ECG (for age > 50)
- Fit / unfit declaration
- Center code and GAMCA / embassy stamp

**What makes it different:** Country-specific format. GCC countries require GAMCA (now Wafid) panel centers only — a non-panel doctor's certificate is rejected. The tests are non-negotiable and a single positive result (HIV, active TB, pregnancy in some countries) means rejection. The report is uploaded electronically to the destination country's health ministry.

**Example:**

```
GAMCA MEDICAL EXAMINATION REPORT
(For Kingdom of Saudi Arabia Employment Visa)

INVESTIGATIONS:

### A8. Pregnancy Certificate

**Where used:** Issued by a gynecologist or general practitioner to confirm pregnancy for: maternity leave application (Maternity Benefit Act, 1961), insurance claims, visa travel (some airlines require it after 28 weeks), and workplace accommodation requests.

**Sections included:**
- Patient name, age, parity (gravida / para)
- Husband's name
- Date of last menstrual period (LMP)
- Date of examination
- Method of confirmation (urine test / ultrasound)
- Ultrasound findings (single / multiple, gestational age, cardiac activity)
- Expected date of delivery (EDD)
- General and abdominal examination findings
- Blood pressure, weight
- Advice (antenatal registration, supplements)
- Doctor's signature and seal

**What makes it different:** Combines clinical confirmation with a legal/administrative purpose. The EDD is the key field for maternity leave planning. Indian maternity leave is 26 weeks for the first two children (Maternity Benefit Amendment Act, 2017), so the certificate must clearly state the EDD. Ultrasound-based dating is preferred over LMP for accuracy.

**Example:**

```

### A9. Vaccination Certificate (COVID / CoWIN)

**Where used:** Issued through the CoWIN platform (cowin.gov.in) for COVID-19 vaccination, and in physical form by hospitals for routine immunization (BCG, OPV, pentavalent, MR, JE). The COVID certificate is mandatory for: international travel, school/college attendance (in some states), workplace entry, and large gatherings. Also used for Yellow Fever vaccination for travel to endemic countries.

**Sections included:**
- Beneficiary name, age, gender
- Beneficiary reference ID
- Aadhaar / photo ID reference (last 4 digits)
- Vaccine name (Covishield / Covaxin / Sputnik V / Corbevax / etc.)
- Dose number (1st / 2nd / precaution)
- Date of each dose
- Vaccination site (left / right deltoid)
- Vaccinator name and batch number
- Vaccination center name and address
- QR code for verification
- Next dose due date (if applicable)
- Issuing authority

**What makes it different:** Digitally signed with a QR code verifiable on the CoWIN portal. The COVID certificate is the only Indian medical certificate with built-in cryptographic verification. International travel requires the certificate to match the passport name exactly. Yellow Fever certificates use the "Carte Jaune" (yellow card) format prescribed by WHO.

### A10. Medical Reimbursement Certificate

**Where used:** Submitted by an employee to their employer (CGHS, central/state government, PSU, private corporate) or to an insurer for reimbursement of medical expenses incurred. Also used for claiming income tax deduction under Section 80D. Requires the original bills, prescriptions, and this certificate from the treating doctor.

**Sections included:**
- Patient name, age, employee ID (if employee)
- Relationship to employee (self / dependent)
- Diagnosis
- Date(s) of consultation / treatment
- Nature of treatment (OPD / IPD / surgery)
- Itemized expenses (consultation, investigations, medicines, procedures, room rent)
- Hospital / clinic name and registration
- Treating doctor's name, qualification, signature, registration number
- Declaration that treatment was necessary
- Hospital seal

**What makes it different:** This is a financial document, not a clinical one. It must itemize expenses and justify medical necessity. CGHS has its own format (CGHS-74). Private insurers require the claim form (signed by the insured) plus this doctor's certificate. The certificate must match the bills exactly — any mismatch leads to rejection.

**Example:```

MEDICAL REIMBURSEMENT CERTIFICATE

### A11. Compensation / Insurance Claim Certificate

**Where used:** Issued by the treating doctor for: motor accident insurance claims (Motor Vehicles Act), Employees' Compensation Act claims (workplace injury), personal accident insurance, and medicolegal cases. Also used for claims under the Prime Minister's Suraksha Bima Yojana. Often paired with an MLC (medicolegal case) register entry and a wound certificate.

**Sections included:**
- Patient name, age, gender, address
- Date and time of examination
- Date, time, and place of incident
- MLC number (if registered)
- Brief history of the incident (in patient's words)
- Injuries found (description, size, location, simple / grievous)
- X-ray / imaging findings
- Treatment given
- Hospitalization period
- Disability / permanent impairment assessment (if applicable)
- Opinion on whether injuries are consistent with the alleged history
- Doctor's name, qualification, signature, registration number
- Hospital seal

**What makes it different:** This is a medicolegal document — it can be produced in court. The doctor may be summoned as a witness. Injuries must be classified as "simple" or "grievous" under Section 320 IPC. The certificate must avoid speculative language. The MLC number links it to the police FIR. Tampering with this document is a criminal offense.

**Example:```
KILPAUK MEDICAL COLLEGE HOSPITAL, CHENNAI
DEPARTMENT OF ORTHOPAEDICS
INSURANCE CLAIM / COMPENSATION CERTIFICATE

MLC No: KMC/MLC/2026/0418

### A12. Organ Donor Certificate

**Where used:** Issued by NOTTO (National Organ and Tissue Transplant Organisation) or the state transplant body (SOTTO) / hospital transplant committee. Two types: (a) living donor — for kidney / partial liver donation to a relative; (b) deceased donor pledge — registered consent for organ donation after brain death. The pledge card is carried by the donor; the living-donor certificate is issued after the transplant authorization committee approval.

**Sections included:**
- Donor name, age, gender, blood group
- Aadhaar / photo ID
- Address
- Type of donation (living / deceased pledge)
- For living donor: recipient name, relationship, organ
- Authorization committee approval number and date
- For pledge: organs consented (kidney, liver, heart, lungs, cornea, skin, etc.)
- Nominee / next of kin name and contact
- Hospital transplant center name and NOTTO registration
- Date of registration / pledge
- Donor signature and witness
- Issuing authority signature and seal

**What makes it different:** Living donation requires a four-step legal process under the Transplantation of Human Organs and Tissues Act, 1994 (amended 2011): donor consent, relationship proof (DNA / affidavit for unrelated), authorization committee approval, and hospital ethics clearance. The pledge card is a consent, not a binding directive — the family's consent is still taken at the time of brain death. NOTTO maintains the national registry.

**Example:```
NOTTO — NATIONAL ORGAN AND TISSUE TRANSPLANT ORGANISATION
(Ministry of Health & Family Welfare, Government of India)

### B1. GP to Specialist Referral

**Where used:** A general practitioner (MBBS / family physician) refers a patient to a specialist (physician, cardiologist, orthopedic, dermatologist, etc.) when the condition is beyond the GP's scope. Common in Indian towns and semi-urban areas where the GP is the first point of contact.

**Sections included:**
- Date
- To: (specialist name and hospital)
- Patient name, age, gender
- Presenting complaint and duration
- Brief history
- Examination findings (vitals, relevant system)
- Investigations done (if any) with results

### B2. Primary to Tertiary Hospital Referral

**Where used:** A primary health center (PHC), community health center (CHC), or district hospital refers a patient to a tertiary center (medical college / apex hospital) for advanced care that is not available locally — e.g., dialysis, cardiac catheterization, neurosurgery, oncology, neonatal intensive care. Common under the PMJAY (Ayushman Bharat) scheme.

**Sections included:**
- Date and time
- From: (referring facility)
- To: (receiving facility)
- Patient name, age, gender, address
- PMJAY / insurance ID (if applicable)
- Presenting complaint and duration
- Brief history and treatment given at the referring facility
- Vitals at the time of referral
- Investigations done with results
- Reason for referral (specific service needed)
- Mode of transport (ambulance / private / 108)
- Accompanying staff (if any)
- Referring doctor's name, signature, registration number
- Receiving facility acknowledgment (filled on arrival)

**What makes it different:** This is a transfer of care, not a consultation request. The patient may not return to the referring facility. Under PMJAY, the referral must mention the PMJAY ID and the package code for the planned procedure. The 108 ambulance service (Dial 108) is often used. The referral must be accepted by the receiving facility — a "referral accepted" stamp is mandatory.

### B3. Emergency Referral

**Where used:** Urgent transfer of a critically ill or injured patient from one facility to another when the referring facility cannot manage the case — e.g., a nursing home without ICU transferring a polytrauma patient to a trauma center, or a district hospital transferring a STEMI patient for primary PCI. Time-critical.

**Sections included:**
- Date and time of referral
- From / To
- Patient name, age, gender
- Brief history (mechanism / onset)
- Vitals and GCS at referral
- Treatment given (drugs, fluids, procedures)
- Reason for urgent transfer (specific intervention needed)
- Mode of transport (108 ambulance with ventilator / private)
- Accompanying staff and their qualification
- Family informed (yes / no)
- Referring doctor's name, signature, registration number
- Receiving facility pre-alert (phone call log)

**What makes it different:** Brevity is essential — written in under 2 minutes. Focuses on what was done and what is needed next, not on history. The receiving facility is pre-alerted by phone. The letter travels with the patient. Often handwritten on a printed proforma. The "golden hour" principle applies — the referral must not delay transfer.

- Patient name, age, gender

- CGHS beneficiary ID
- Patient name, age, gender
- Relation to beneficiary (self / dependent)
- Wellness Centre of registration
- Date of issue
- Diagnosis / complaint
- Service requested (consultation / investigation / admission)

### B7. PMJAY Referral

**Where used:** A PMJAY (Ayushman Bharat Pradhan Mantri Jan Arogya Yojana) empaneled hospital refers a beneficiary to another empaneled hospital (usually higher level) for a service not available at the referring hospital. **Sections included:**
- PMJAY ID / Aadhaar number
- Patient name, age, gender
- Family ID
- State / district
- Referring hospital (with HFR ID)
- Receiving hospital (with HFR ID)
- Date and time
- Diagnosis
- PMJAY package code(s) requested
- Reason for referral (service not available)
- Treatment given so far
- Vitals at referral
- Referring doctor's name and signature
- PMJAY coordinator's signature

- Patient name, age, gender

### B9. Physiotherapy Referral

**Where used:** A doctor (orthopedic, neurologist, physician, GP) refers a patient to a physiotherapist for rehabilitation — post-fracture, post-stroke, post-joint replacement, chronic back pain, Bell's palsy, etc. The physiotherapist designs the protocol based on this referral.

**Sections included:**
- Date
- Patient name, age, gender
- Diagnosis
- Brief relevant history (surgery / injury date)
- Current status (pain, range of motion, weakness)
- Goal of physiotherapy (pain relief / strengthening / mobility / gait training)
- Precautions (weight-bearing status, cardiac limits)
- Number of sessions advised
- Doctor's name, signature, registration number

**What makes it different:** Specifies the goal and precautions, not the exact exercises — the physiotherapist decides the technique. Weight-bearing status (non / partial / full) is critical for post-fracture referrals. For stroke patients, the stage (flaccid / spastic) guides the approach. ### B10. Mental Health Referral

**Where used:** A GP, physician, or pediatrician refers a patient to a psychiatrist, clinical psychologist, or counselor. Also used by schools (via school counselor) and by courts (under the Mental Healthcare Act, 2017). The referral must respect patient confidentiality and consent.

**Sections included:**
- Date
- Patient name, age, gender
- Presenting psychological / behavioral complaint
- Duration
- Impact on daily functioning
- Relevant medical history (thyroid, seizures, substance use)
- Any risk assessment (self-harm / harm to others) — if known
- Reason for referral (diagnosis / therapy / medication)
- Doctor's name, signature, registration number

**What makes it different:** Stigma around mental health in India means referrals are often worded neutrally ("for evaluation and counseling") to protect the patient. ### C1. SOAP Note (Standard)

**Where used:** The most widely used OPD note format in Indian private practice and corporate hospitals. Applicable across specialties. Provides a structured, reproducible record of each consultation.

**Sections included:**
- **S**ubjective: patient's complaints, history, associated symptoms, in patient's words
- **O**bjective: vitals, physical examination findings, investigation results
- **A**ssessment: diagnosis (provisional / differential), problem list
- **P**lan: medications, investigations ordered, procedures, advice, follow-up

**What makes it different:** The four-letter structure forces a clear separation of patient-reported data (S) from doctor-observed data (O). The Assessment is a synthesis, not a repetition. The Plan is actionable. Indian doctors often compress the SOAP into 4–6 lines in busy OPDs, but the structure is preserved. EHR systems (Practo, Lybrate, HealthPlix) use SOAP as the default template.

### C5. ICU Daily Progress Note (Systems-Based)

- Date, day of ICU stay, bed number
- 24-hour summary (one line)
- Systems review:
  - Neuro: GCS, pupils, sedation
  - CVS: rhythm, BP, inotropes (dose)
  - RS: ventilator settings, ABG, SpO2
  - abdomen: feeds, bowel, distension
  - Renal: urine output, RRT if any
  - Others: lines, drains, cultures
- Investigations (today's)
- Issues / active problems
- Plan for the day
- Consultant's note and signature

**Sections included:**
- Time of arrival, time of note
- Mode of arrival (walk-in / 108 / private vehicle)
- Chief complaint (one line)
- Brief vitals (T, P, BP, RR, SpO2, GCS)
- Focused examination (the relevant system only)
- Immediate actions (IV line, O2, drugs given)
- Investigations sent (STAT)
- Provisional diagnosis
- Disposition (admit / discharge / refer / observe)
- Doctor's signature

**What makes it different:** Brevity and speed. No history of present illness, no review of systems. The note is a snapshot of the patient at arrival and what was done in the first few minutes. ### C10. Teleconsultation Note

**Sections included:**
- Date, time, mode (audio / video / chat)
- Platform name
- Patient name, age, gender
- Location (for emergency referral if needed)
- Chief complaint
- History (as narrated)
- Any self-reported vitals / readings (BP, sugar, temp)
- Any uploaded reports (photos of previous reports)
- Assessment
- Advice (medicines, investigations, lifestyle)
- Red flags — when to seek in-person care
- Follow-up
- Doctor's digital signature and registration number

Indian telemedicine guidelines (Telemedicine Practice Guidelines 2020) require the doctor's registration number and a clear statement of the mode. **Where used:** When a patient returns for review of a previously diagnosed condition — hypertension, diabetes, hypothyroidism, post-treatment review, or chronic disease monitoring. The note compares today's status with the last visit.

**Sections included:**
- Date
- Patient name, MRD number
- Diagnosis (carried forward)
- Last visit date and key parameters
- Today's complaint (if any) / "no new complaint"
- Today's vitals and relevant examination
- Investigations done since last visit (with results)
- Comparison (better / same / worse)
- Changes to treatment (if any)
- Next follow-up

**What makes it different:** Shorter than a first-visit note. The focus is on the trend — is the BP controlled, is the HbA1c down, is the TSH in range? **Where used:** When a specialist (cardiologist, neurologist, nephrologist) is called to see an inpatient admitted under another unit. The specialist examines the patient, reviews the file, and writes their opinion in the patient's bed-head ticket. This is the "cross-consult" note.

**Sections included:**
- Date and time
- Specialist's name and department
- Patient name, bed, MRD number
- "Thank you for referring" line
- Brief relevant history (from the file)
- Focused examination by the specialist
- Investigations reviewed
- Specialist's opinion / diagnosis
- Recommendations (medications, investigations, procedure)
- Signature

### D1. Pre-Anesthetic Checkup (PAC)

**Where used:** Conducted by the anesthesiologist 1–2 days before elective surgery (or on the same day for emergency surgery) to assess fitness for anesthesia. The PAC is mandatory — surgery cannot proceed without it.

**Sections included:**
- Date, time
- Patient name, age, gender, weight, height, BMI
- Proposed surgery
- Surgical unit / consultant
- Presenting complaint and brief history
- Past medical / surgical / anesthetic history
- Drug history and allergies
- Personal history (smoking, alcohol)
- Airway assessment (Mallampati, mouth opening, neck movement, dentition)
- Systemic examination (CVS, RS, CNS)
- Vitals
- Investigations reviewed (CBC, renal, hepatic, sugar, ECG, X-ray, echo if indicated)
- Co-morbidities and optimization status
- ASA physical status classification (I–VI)
- Anesthesia plan (GA / spinal / epidural / regional / combined)
- Special concerns / anticipated difficulty
- Consent for anesthesia (separate from surgical consent)
- Fasting status
- Anesthesiologist's signature

**What makes it different:** Focuses on anesthesia fitness, not surgical fitness. The ASA grade (I = healthy, II = mild systemic disease, III = severe systemic disease, IV = life-threatening, V = moribund, VI = organ donor) determines the risk and is recorded. The airway assessment (Mallampati) is unique to PAC — it predicts difficult intubation. Indian PACs follow the IBA (Indian Board of Anesthesiology) / ASA (American Society of Anesthesiologists) guidelines.

**Example:```

- No prior surgery. No prior anesthesia.

Vitals: BP 132/80, HR 78, RR 16, SpO2 98% RA, Temp 98.4°F

Investigations:
Hb 12.4 | TLC 7,200 | Platelets 2.6 lakh | BT 2' CT 4'
Blood group: A positive

### D2. Pre-Op Surgical Note

**Where used:** Written by the surgeon before the operation, usually the evening before or the morning of surgery. States the surgical plan, indication, and approach. Part of the patient's file.

**Sections included:**
- Date
- Patient name, age, gender, MRD number
- Bed / ward
- Working diagnosis
- Indication for surgery (with brief justification)
- Planned procedure (named)
- Approach (open / laparoscopic / robotic / endoscopic)
- Side (for bilateral organs — right / left)
- Expected duration
- Consent status (obtained / pending)
- Special requirements (blood, equipment, implants, frozen section)
- Positioning
- Antibiotic prophylaxis (drug, dose, timing)
- Surgeon's signature

**What makes it different:** This is the surgical plan, not the operative record. It must match the consent form exactly — any deviation is a medico-legal issue. The side (right / left) is explicitly written and marked on the patient's body with a marker. Blood requirements are flagged in advance. Indian hospitals follow the WHO surgical safety checklist, which begins with this note.

**Example:```

### D3. Intra-Op Note (Brief)

**Where used:** A short operative record written immediately after surgery, often by the surgeon or assistant, while the details are fresh. Used for minor and intermediate procedures, or as a preliminary note before the detailed note is typed.

**Sections included:**
- Date, time of start and end
- Patient name, MRD number
- Surgeon, assistant, anesthesiologist
- Procedure performed (named)
- Anesthesia type
- Position, draping
- Findings (brief)
- Steps (one line each, key steps only)
- Specimen sent (if any)
- Blood loss
- Drain
- Closure
- Patient condition at end
- Surgeon's signature

**What makes it different:** One page or less. Captures the essentials — what was found, what was done, how the patient tolerated it. The detailed note (D4) is typed later. Indian government hospitals often use this brief format as the only record due to volume; corporate hospitals use it as a preliminary note pending the typed detailed note.

### D4. Intra-Op Note (Detailed, with Steps)

**Where used:** The full operative record, typed and signed by the surgeon. Required for all major surgeries. Stored in the patient's file and the hospital's OT register. This is the definitive surgical document.

**Sections included:**
- Date, time of incision, time of closure, total duration
- Patient name, age, gender, MRD number
- Surgeon, assistant(s), anesthesiologist, scrub nurse
- Pre-op diagnosis
- Post-op diagnosis (if different)
- Procedure performed (named, with laterality)
- Anesthesia type and any issues
- Position, skin preparation, draping
- Incision (described)
- Step-by-step operative findings and actions (narrative)
- Specimen(s) sent (HPE, culture, frozen section)
- Hemostasis
- Drain (type, size, location)
- Closure (layer by layer)
- Blood loss (estimated)
- Replacement (fluids, blood)
- Urine output
- Intra-op complications (if any)
- Patient condition at end
- Antibiotics given
- Surgeon's signature

**What makes it different:** This is the most detailed document in the medical record — often 2–4 pages. Every step is described so another surgeon could reconstruct the operation. Findings are distinguished from actions. Complications are explicitly stated (or "none"). Indian corporate hospitals require this typed note within 24 hours of surgery. It is the primary document in any surgical negligence case.

### D5. Post-Op Note (Immediate)

**Where used:** Written in the recovery room / PACU (Post-Anesthesia Care Unit) immediately after surgery, before the patient is shifted to the ward or ICU. Documents the patient's emergence from anesthesia and immediate post-op status.

**Sections included:**
- Date, time of arrival in recovery
- Patient name, MRD number
- Procedure performed
- Anesthesia type
- Time of reversal / extubation
- Vitals on arrival (BP, HR, RR, SpO2, Temp)
- Consciousness level (awake / drowsy / responding to commands)
- Pain score
- Surgical site: dressing (dry / soaked), drain (output, color)
- IV fluids running
- Urine output (if catheterized)
- Any immediate complication (nausea, shivering, desaturation)
- Disposition (ward / ICU / HDU)
- Recovery room score (Aldrete)
- Doctor's signature

**What makes it different:** Focuses on anesthesia recovery, not the surgery. The Aldrete score (5 parameters: activity, respiration, circulation, consciousness, oxygen) determines when the patient can leave recovery — a score of 9–10 is required. The note is brief and time-stamped every 15 minutes. Any complication in the first hour (bleeding, desaturation, hypotension) is caught here.

**Example:```

### D6. Post-Op Orders

**Where used:** Written by the surgeon (or anesthesiologist) immediately after surgery, as a standing order sheet for the nursing staff to follow for the next 24 hours. Placed at the bed-head.

**Sections included:**
- Date, time
- Patient name, bed, MRD number
- Procedure performed
- Diagnosis
- Monitoring (vitals frequency, SpO2, urine output)
- Position (supine / semi-Fowler / lateral)
- Diet (NPO / sips / clear fluids / soft — progression)
- IV fluids (type, rate)
- Medications:
  - Antibiotics (drug, dose, frequency, duration)
  - Analgesics (drug, dose, frequency, SOS)
  - Antiemetics
  - PPI / GI protection
  - DVT prophylaxis (LMWH, stockings)
  - Specific drugs (insulin, antihypertensives)
- Wound care (dressing, drain monitoring)
- Mobilization (bed rest / bedside / ambulation)
- Investigations (CBC, renal, sugar — when)
- Call doctor if (red flags)
- Surgeon's signature

**What makes it different:** This is a directive to nurses, not a note. Each order is actionable. The "call doctor if" section is critical — it defines the red flags (BP < 90, HR > 120, temp > 101, soakage, reduced urine). Indian hospitals use a printed order sheet; the surgeon ticks and fills blanks. Orders are reviewed and rewritten daily.

### D7. Surgery Discharge Note

**Where used:** The discharge summary for a surgical patient, typed and handed to the patient at discharge. Combines the medical and surgical course. Required for insurance claims, follow-up at another hospital, and medicolegal records.

**Sections included:**
- Hospital header, MRD number, date of admission, date of discharge
- Patient demographics
- Consultant and unit
- Presenting complaint
- History of present illness
- Past medical / surgical history
- Examination at admission
- Investigations
- Diagnosis (pre-op and post-op)
- Procedure performed (with date)
- Operative findings (brief)
- Post-op course (complications, if any)
- Hospital course summary
- Condition at discharge
- Discharge medications (with dose, duration)
- Advice (diet, activity, wound care, stoma care)
- Follow-up date
- Review instructions (when to come back urgently)
- Discharge summary typed by and signed by consultant

**What makes it different:** Combines the medical and surgical narrative in one document. The operative findings and post-op course are unique to surgical discharges. The "advice" section is detailed (wound care, stoma care, activity restrictions) because the patient manages these at home. Indian insurance claims require this document. The discharge summary is often 2–3 pages.

- Procedure performed
- Indication
- Surgeon / doctor
- Assistant
- Anesthesia (local — drug, concentration, volume)

- Aseptic preparation

**What makes it different:** Short (half a page). Done under local anesthesia, no OT booking. The note serves as both the operative record and the prescription. **Where used:** For upper GI endoscopy (UGIE), colonoscopy, sigmoidoscopy, ERCP, bronchoscopy, cystoscopy. Performed in the endoscopy suite under sedation or local anesthesia. The note is generated by the endoscopist immediately after the procedure, often with photos.

**Sections included:**
- Date, time
- Patient name, age, gender, MRD number
- Procedure performed
- Indication
- Endoscopist
- Assistant / nurse
- Anesthesia / sedation (drug, dose)
- Endoscope model
- Patient preparation (fasting / bowel prep)
- Tolerance (good / fair / poor)
- Findings (organ by organ — esophagus, stomach, duodenum for UGIE; rectum, sigmoid, descending, transverse, ascending, cecum, terminal ileum for colonoscopy)
- Interventions done (biopsy, polypectomy, dilation, stent)
- Specimen sent
- Complications (if any)
- Photos (attached / saved)
- Post-procedure advice
- Follow-up
- Endoscopist's signature

Photos are integral — Indian endoscopy systems (Olympus, Pentax, Fujifilm) capture and store them. Biopsy sites are specified. The note is shorter than a surgical operative note but follows a strict template.

### D10. Anesthesia Record

**Where used:** Maintained by the anesthesiologist (or anesthesia technician) throughout the surgery. A graphical chart with vitals plotted every 5 minutes, drugs given, fluids, urine output, and events. The definitive anesthesia document.

**Sections included:**
- Patient name, age, gender, weight, MRD number
- Date, procedure
- Anesthesiologist(s)
- ASA grade
- Pre-op vitals
- Anesthesia technique (GA / spinal / epidural / regional)
- Drugs given (induction, maintenance, reversal) — drug, dose, time
- Airway: tube size, LMA, mask; intubation ease
- Ventilator settings (mode, FiO2, tidal volume, rate, PEEP)
- Vitals chart (5-minutely): BP, HR, SpO2, EtCO2, Temp
- Fluids: crystalloid, colloid, blood — type and volume
- Blood loss (estimated)
- Urine output
- Events: incision, intubation, extubation, any complication
- Drugs (intra-op): analgesics, muscle relaxants, vasoactive drugs
- Positioning and duration
- Condition at end (extubated / ventilated)
- Anesthesiologist's signature

**What makes it different:** This is a graphical chart, not a narrative. Vitals are plotted on a pre-printed grid. The 5-minute interval is mandatory — it shows trends, not just snapshots. EtCO2 (end-tidal CO2) is unique to anesthesia records. The chart is medico-legally critical — any gap in the record is scrutinized in case of adverse events. Indian anesthesia records follow the IBA template.

### D11. Consent Form (Operation)

**Where used:** Signed by the patient (or guardian / next of kin) before any surgical or invasive procedure. Mandatory under the Indian Contract Act, Consumer Protection Act, and Supreme Court judgments (Samira Kohli vs Dr. Prabha Manhan, 2018). Without valid consent, the surgery is technically assault.

**Sections included:**
- Hospital header
- Patient name, age, gender, MRD number
- Procedure named (in English and local language)
- Indication
- Name of primary surgeon
- Type of anesthesia
- Statement that the procedure, its purpose, benefits, and alternatives have been explained
- Specific risks and complications listed (bleeding, infection, anesthetic risks, organ injury, conversion, recurrence, need for re-operation, rare serious events)
- Statement that the patient has understood and has no further questions
- Consent for anesthesia (separate or combined)
- Consent for blood transfusion (if applicable)
- Consent for biopsy / HPE
- Consent for photography (if applicable)
- Signature of patient (or LAR — legally acceptable representative)
- Signature of witness (with name and relation)
- Signature of surgeon
- Signature of interpreter (if language barrier)
- Date and time

**What makes it different:** This is a legal document, not a medical one. The Samira Kohli judgment requires that consent be "real and informed" — the patient must understand the nature of the procedure, alternatives, and risks. Consent must be taken by the doctor performing the procedure, not a junior. Blanket consents ("consent for surgery in general") are invalid. The form must be in a language the patient understands. Indian courts have held that absence of a properly filled consent form is evidence of negligence.

**Example:```

### D12. Surgical Checklist (WHO Surgical Safety)

**Where used:** The WHO Surgical Safety Checklist is mandatory in all Indian operating theatres (NABH accreditation requirement). Completed in three phases: Sign In (before anesthesia), Time Out (before incision), Sign Out (before patient leaves OT). The checklist is read aloud by the circulating nurse and confirmed by the team.

**Sections included:**

**Sign In (before induction of anesthesia):**
- Patient confirmed (name, procedure, site, consent)
- Site marked
- Anesthesia safety check (equipment, drugs)
- Pulse oximeter on and functioning
- Known allergies
- Difficult airway / aspiration risk
- Risk of > 500 mL blood loss (or in children, > 7 mL/kg)

**Time Out (before skin incision):**
- All team members introduce themselves by name and role
- Surgeon, anesthesia, nurse confirm: patient, site, procedure
- Antibiotic prophylaxis given within last 60 minutes
- Imaging displayed (if relevant)
- Critical events: surgeon reviews anticipated critical steps
- Anesthesia reviews patient-specific concerns
- Nursing reviews sterility, equipment issues

**Sign Out (before patient leaves OR):**
- Nurse confirms: procedure recorded, instrument / swab / needle counts correct, specimen labeled
- Equipment problems
- Surgeon, anesthesia, nurse review key concerns for recovery
- Disposition (ward / HDU / ICU)

**What makes it different:** This is a verbal, team-based checklist, not a doctor's note. It is read aloud — silence is not acceptable. The three phases correspond to the three highest-risk moments in surgery (anesthesia induction, incision, and handover). NABH (National Accreditation Board for Hospitals) audits compliance. The checklist has been shown to reduce surgical mortality and morbidity by ~40% globally. Indian government hospitals are increasingly adopting it.
